# The Euclidian

## Zenith-Optimized Computational Speckle Imaging Platform

---

# Overview

The Euclidian is an experimental computational astronomy platform designed around short-exposure speckle imaging, drift-based acquisition, and geometric computational reconstruction.

The project explores an alternative observational philosophy that intentionally shifts complexity away from ultra-precise mechanical tracking and toward:

* Statistical reconstruction
* Computational stabilization
* Rotational registration
* Drift-based acquisition
* Geometric correction
* Autonomous operation
* Closed-loop motion control

Rather than following the traditional long-exposure astrophotography model.

The platform combines:

* Vertical observation geometry
* Alt-Azi positioning
* Closed-loop hybrid stepper systems
* Speckle imaging methods
* Computational rotational correction
* Drift stacking workflows
* Autonomous observation control

into a unified experimental imaging architecture.

# Foundational Optical Principle

The Euclidian is partially based on the observation that light arriving from extremely distant astronomical targets behaves as a highly collimated wavefront by the time it reaches the observation system.

For targets at interstellar distances, incoming light can be treated as effectively parallel across the aperture scale of the instrument.

The project explores whether this naturally collimated incoming light geometry can be leveraged within a computational imaging framework that combines:

* Speckle imaging
* Drift acquisition
* Statistical reconstruction
* Computational stabilization
* Geometric registration
* Rotational compensation

Rather than relying exclusively on conventional long-exposure optical amplification methods.

The Euclidian treats:

* Atmosphere
* Optical geometry
* Mechanical structure
* Motion systems
* Reconstruction software

as a coupled observational system.

# Core Design Philosophy

## Computational Recovery Over Mechanical Perfection

Traditional astrophotography systems typically depend on:

* Precision equatorial tracking
* Long exposure integration
* Extremely low periodic error
* Mechanical compensation of Earth rotation

The Euclidian instead investigates whether modern computational methods can offset many of these traditional mechanical requirements.

The platform intentionally prioritizes:

* Structural rigidity
* Repeatable geometry
* Short exposure acquisition
* Statistical reconstruction
* Computational correction
* Autonomous repeatability

over ultra-precise continuous tracking.

---

# Zenith-Optimized Vertical Architecture

The latest Euclidian iteration adopts a vertically oriented observation geometry.

This configuration was selected to address several limitations associated with conventional Alt-Azi telescope systems operating near zenith, including:

* Rapid azimuth acceleration
* Rotational instability
* Dynamic asymmetry
* Variable gravitational flexure
* Harmonic instability

By orienting the instrument vertically, the platform benefits from:

* Symmetric gravitational loading
* Improved torsional rigidity
* Reduced cantilever flex
* Improved zenith accessibility
* More stable mechanical behavior
* Reduced dynamic imbalance

The architecture intentionally favors computational correction and statistical reconstruction over continuous precision tracking.

---

# Why Zenith Matters

The Euclidian is specifically optimized for short-exposure computational imaging near zenith.

Atmospheric quality generally improves near zenith because the optical path through the atmosphere is minimized.

Advantages include:

* Reduced atmospheric distortion
* Improved coherence stability
* Reduced scintillation
* Improved speckle consistency
* Improved reconstruction quality

Traditional Alt-Azi systems often struggle near zenith due to rotational and kinematic instability.

The Euclidian instead embraces zenith operation as a primary observational regime.

# Mount Architecture

## Alt-Azi Positioning System

The platform uses an Alt-Azi motion system optimized for:

* Mechanical simplicity
* Structural rigidity
* Autonomous operation
* Compact mass distribution
* Reduced rotational inertia
* Computational correction workflows

Unlike traditional telescope mounts, the system is not primarily designed for long-duration continuous precision tracking.

Instead, the platform operates using:

* Drift acquisition
* Frame extraction
* Computational registration
* Statistical reconstruction

---

# Motion Control System

## Closed-Loop Hybrid Servo Architecture

Current motion hardware:

* 17HS08-1004D-E1K closed-loop stepper motors
* CL42T closed-loop drivers

The system behaves as a hybrid servo architecture using encoder feedback for positional correction.

Advantages include:

* Encoder-based correction
* Stall detection
* Reduced resonance behavior
* Improved low-speed smoothness
* Improved positional repeatability
* Improved autonomous reliability
* Better vibration control

The platform is optimized for repeatability and structural stability rather than classical high-precision astronomical tracking.

# Optical Geometry

The current optical configuration operates with approximately:

* 400 mm projection distance from the primary optical element

This geometry was selected to balance:

* Structural stability
* Vibration tolerance
* Atmospheric seeing limitations
* Mechanical rigidity
* Reconstruction efficiency
* Reduced sensitivity to tracking error

The relatively compact optical geometry helps preserve speckle coherence while reducing amplification of structural instability.

# Imaging Methodology

## Speckle Imaging

The Euclidian uses short exposure imaging to capture atmospheric speckle patterns.

The reconstruction pipeline investigates:

* Speckle interferometry
* Lucky imaging techniques
* Frame selection
* Drift stacking
* Fourier-domain reconstruction
* Computational stabilization
* Geometric registration

The project explores how far computational imaging can compensate for limitations traditionally solved through expensive mechanical systems.

---

# Drift-Based Acquisition

The Euclidian does not rely on continuous long-duration precision tracking.

Instead, the platform positions ahead of a target and allows natural sky motion to drift through the field of view.

Advantages include:

* Reduced tracking complexity
* Reduced sensitivity to periodic error
* Lower mechanical requirements
* Improved structural simplicity
* Better compatibility with statistical reconstruction
* Reduced dependence on equatorial alignment

This methodology is central to the overall architecture.

---

# Computational Rotational Compensation

Because the platform uses an Alt-Azi geometry, rotational compensation is required.

The current development direction prioritizes computational rotational correction using:

* Video frame extraction
* Rotational registration
* Drift alignment
* Software geometric correction
* Statistical frame reconstruction

The project is actively investigating whether software-based rotational compensation can fully replace dedicated mechanical camera derotation systems.

The architecture intentionally prioritizes computational correction before introducing additional mechanical complexity.

---

# Geometric Sky Calibration

The Euclidian does not rely on traditional precision polar alignment.

Instead, the platform is being designed around:

* Vertical gravitational reference geometry
* Encoder-based positional feedback
* Plate-solving refinement
* Software-derived sky transforms
* Computational calibration

The system uses the sky itself as a geometric calibration reference.

This allows:

* Faster setup
* Improved autonomous operation
* Reduced mechanical alignment complexity
* Self-correcting coordinate mapping
